        <div class="container">
            <h2>STDEV.P & STDEV.S: Standard Deviation</h2>

            <p>
                **Standard Deviation** is a measure of the amount of variation or dispersion of a set of values. A low standard deviation indicates that the values tend to be close to the mean (average) of the set, while a high standard deviation indicates that the values are spread out over a wider range.
            </p>

            <p>
                **Key Difference:** The choice between `STDEV.P` and `STDEV.S` depends on whether your data represents the entire population or just a sample.
            </p>

            ---

            <div class="formula-section">
                <h3>1. STDEV.P (Population Standard Deviation)</h3>

                <p>Calculates the standard deviation based on the **entire population** given as arguments.</p>

                <div class="formula-syntax">
                    <code>=STDEV.P(number1, [number2], ...)</code>
                </div>

                <ul class="argument-list">
                    <li>
                        <strong>number1, [number2], ...</strong>: The numbers representing the entire population. This is typically a range of cells (e.g., `A2:A100`).
                    </li>
                </ul>

                <h4>Example üí° (STDEV.P)</h4>
                <p>
                    You have sales data for **every single store** your company owns (the entire population). You use `STDEV.P` on the sales figures:
                </p>
                <div class="formula-syntax">
                    <code>=STDEV.P(C2:C100)</code>
                </div>
            </div>

            ---

            <div class="formula-section">
                <h3>2. STDEV.S (Sample Standard Deviation)</h3>

                <p>Calculates the standard deviation based on a **sample** of the population. This is the **most common** scenario in statistical analysis.</p>

                <div class="formula-syntax">
                    <code>=STDEV.S(number1, [number2], ...)</code>
                </div>
                
                <p class="note">
                    **Technical Note:** `STDEV.S` uses $n-1$ in the denominator (Bessel's correction) to provide an unbiased estimate of the population standard deviation, making it more appropriate for samples.
                </p>

                <ul class="argument-list">
                    <li>
                        <strong>number1, [number2], ...</strong>: A numerical **sample** of the population.
                    </li>
                </ul>

                <h4>Example üí° (STDEV.S)</h4>
                <p>
                    You have sales data for a random selection of **30 stores** (a sample of the population). You use `STDEV.S` on the sales figures:
                </p>
                <div class="formula-syntax">
                    <code>=STDEV.S(C2:C31)</code>
                </div>
            </div>
